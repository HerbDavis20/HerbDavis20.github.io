Evaluating Bitcoin: The Transition Towards Financial Legitimacy

Aims:
This project aims to provide an insight into how the perception of Bitcoin has changed as an asset over time. From Bitcoin's inception in 2009, there has been drastic changes in the way people view Bitcoin. In it's early years, traditional investors had significant reservations on the validity of Bitcoin due to its erratic price volatility and domination in dark web transactions. Now, Bitcoin is a common asset among some of the largest funds and asset managers, this year being the first time a Bitcoin spot ETF has been approved by the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commision (SEC).

Through this project I aim to take a data driven approach using multiple metrics and statistical methods to reveal the changes in the fundamental value of Bitcoin, to both institutional and retail investors.

To begin with, I looked at a broader view of the historic price movements of the VIX volatility index and the price of Bitcoin to discover any prominent trends in the data. The data for this chart comes from the FRED API and is embedded directily into the vega spec to allow it to remain live and update daily with the data.

The VIX volatility index is derived from combining weighted prices of the S&P500 put and call option prices for the next 30 days. It predicts the likely range of movement in US eqity markets and is often regarded as the 'fear' index. The VIX represents a good indication of the current equity market sentiment.

I thought this would be a useful comparison to make as conventionally, during times of high market volatility, investors move from stocks into 'safe haven' assets like gold or U.S. Treasury bonds to mitigate risk. If there is a similar trend developing with Bitcoin, it shows that investors are beginning to view it as more of a valid and valuable asset from what it once was.

Challenges:

As most of my data used in this project is daily price data, the majority of the challenges I came across were in cleaning and manupulating the data in python to extract as much information from it as possible. In particular, working with dates caused many issues due to the different formats that dates come in. To resolve this, I began to use pd.to_datetime function on all dates before beginning my analysis as this would prevent issues later.

Another issue I came across was finding live price data. Many of the API's that are available were behind paywalls and only allowed minimal data to be extracted for free. The FRED API works well and is easy to use however lacks a lot of key data for my project. I discovered the yfinance package late into my project but this was an effective tool in sourcing live financial data.

Another challenge I came across was values in the dataset not being stored as numbers so when it came to working with the data I was getting a lot of NaN outputs. To solve this I made sure to convert all the data into floats or integers using .astype(float)

When filtering values out of a dataframe, I also learnt the importance of resetting the index back to zero to prevent issues when merging dataframes later on. During my analysis in python, with so many stages of filtering and calculations, I found it useful to make new dataframes for different stages of anaylsis, rather than continue updataing the same dataframe. This was better for experimentation and made errors easier to find and resolve.
